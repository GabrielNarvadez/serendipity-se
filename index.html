Chapter 01 · First Reaction

Sixty tabs.
Three tables.

My first instinct opening the workbook: "this is complex." Then I went into the raw dumps. Every single tab — consolidated report, sector rollup, rep dashboards, exec summary, AR trends — is a pivot of three underlying datasets.

The complexity is in the presentation, not the data.

60+TABS

1Sales Transactions

Every ERP row: customer × product × month × gallons × amount. The atomic unit.

2Budget

Per customer, per month, gallons + dollars. Source of every "vs Budget" number.

3Customer → Sector

Foodservice · Retail · Private Label · Bulk · PL Foodservice. A manual lookup — lives nowhere in the ERPs.

Chapter 02 · Reframing

The real problem.

If we solve the problem as briefed, we'll build the wrong thing. Here's the reframe I'd bring to the first meeting.

How it sounds

Move a spreadsheet into HubSpot

Implies 1:1 port. Ends with us rebuilding 60 tabs of pivot tables inside a CRM that can't render them, and a client disappointed it doesn't look like Excel.

What it actually is

BI-style rollup inside a CRM

Replicate the numbers and the filters. Not the layout. HubSpot becomes the source of truth; the exec summary becomes a dashboard with a month selector, not a PDF.

Chapter 04 · What I Wouldn't Do

Deals ≠ transactions.

The rubric called out someone naming a deal "gallons sold." That's the anti-pattern. Every decision downstream gets worse if we model this wrong.

DON'T
Anti-pattern

Deal: "Gallons Sold"

Forces completed shipments into a pipeline object built for active sales motions. Inflates deal counts, breaks forecasting, confuses the sales team about what's real pipeline vs. history.

  • 1Deals have stages. These are closed shipments — no stage, no close date, no motion.
  • 2Deals are singular. One customer buys 400 line items a month. We'd flood the Deal pipeline.
  • 3Deals skew reporting. "Deals closed this month" suddenly means "invoice lines billed" — every other HubSpot dashboard lies.
  • 4Custom objects exist for this. That's what they're for. Use them.

Likely next question · Why not just Products?

Catalog vs. event.
They're different jobs.

If Deals are wrong, shouldn't we just use HubSpot's native Products? Good instinct — but Products alone can't hold transactional data. Here's the clean split.

📦
Native · Product
The catalog
  • One record per SKU ("ORANGE 4/1GL PERRICONE")
  • Static attributes — price, cost, flavor, UOM
  • Defines what we sell
  • Rarely changes after setup
~40 records · stable
×
🧾
Custom · Sales Transaction
The event
  • One record per customer × product × month
  • Dynamic — gallons, qty, rate, amount, date
  • Defines what was sold, when, to whom
  • Associated to both Company and Product
~14,500 records / year · grows monthly

Product models the thing. Sales Transaction models the event.

HubSpot's native alternative — Line Items — only exists as a child of a Deal or Quote. Using them would force us back into Deals, the anti-pattern we just ruled out. Sales Transaction as a standalone custom object is the clean escape.

Chapter 07 · Rebuilding The Report

Four layers,
stacked.

HubSpot isn't a BI tool. So we build it in layers, each doing the part it's best at.

01

Native custom reports

Top customers, sector performance, plant comparison, rep dashboards, AR aging. Month filter replaces "Select Here." Real-time once API is live.

Out of the box
02

Calculated properties

Variance vs Budget, YoY %, Mix %, ASP. One-line calculations on the custom objects.

Derived
03

Scheduled workflows

Nightly rollup of YTD gallons & sales onto Company. Keeps dashboards fast, avoids re-aggregating on every page load.

Pre-computed
04

Exec summary — client's call

Option A: HubSpot dashboard (recommended). Option B: external BI tool (Hex / Sigma / Sheets) for exact PDF layout. I'd let them choose — it's a preference question, not a technical one.

Decision point

Chapter 12 · Deep-Level Thinking

Naming compounds.

These are the labels the Account Strategist will repeat to the client for the next year. Get them right on day one.

Thing
Bad name
Good name
Why
Atomic sales record
"Gallons Sold" deal
Sales Transaction
It's a transaction, not a deal. "Gallons" is one metric on it, not its identity.
Budget record
"Budget Deal"
Budget Line
Matches finance's mental model. Parallel to "Sales Transaction."
Batchmaster pipeline
"BM Sync"
Beaumont Plant Ingestion
Client thinks in plants, not ERP brand names. Future-proofs if they switch ERPs.
Sector field
"Type"
Channel / Sector
"Type" is meaningless. "Channel" is what their sales team actually says.
Custom dashboard
"Sales Report"
Consolidated Sales Performance — [Month]
Matches the Excel title they already know. Reduces training friction.
